## 1. The Business Concept

### What the Product Is

A **site-specific cUAS (counter-Unmanned Aerial System) simulation and configuration tool** that:

- Ingests site data (LiDAR terrain mapping, site boundaries, airspace constraints)
- Models sensor and effector coverage across a 3D site environment
- Identifies coverage gaps, dead zones, and optimal sensor placement
- Simulates system configurations against threat profiles (drone type, approach vectors, speed)
- Outputs a professional, report-quality deliverable suitable for use in defence and civilian procurement proposals

### The Gap Being Addressed

Hardware-first cUAS vendors (DroneShield, Dedrone, Acacia, etc.) sell systems — they don't sell independent, vendor-agnostic site planning analysis. Site survey work done today is largely manual, experience-dependent, and not reproducible in a standardised format. A simulation tool that de-risks configuration decisions before hardware is procured — and validates proposals with data — is a genuine, underserved gap in the Australian market.

### Core Value Proposition

- Reduces risk of wrong-configuration errors (expensive to fix post-deployment)
- Strengthens technical proposals with data-backed coverage analysis
- Vendor-agnostic positioning differentiates from hardware vendor software
- Faster, more repeatable site analysis vs. manual methods
- Enables "what-if" comparison between competing system configurations

## 2. Market Context — Australia's cUAS Landscape

### The Regulatory Moment

The timing is favourable. The Australian Government has enacted the **Defence Amendment (Counter-UXS Measures) Regulations 2025**, expanding Defence authority to detect, disable, or destroy drones threatening ADF personnel and facilities. Simultaneously, the **Land 156 Standing Offer Panel (LOE3)** was established in January 2026 to enable site-by-site procurement of cUAS capability across Defence and whole-of-government sites.

The government has committed **AUD $1.3 billion** for counter-drone capabilities over the next decade. Drone flight activity in Australia is forecast to grow from ~1.5 million flights in 2023 to **60 million by 2043**.

### Project Land 156 — Structure

Land 156 is structured across multiple Lines of Effort (LOEs):

| LOE | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| LOE1 | Systems integration (won by Leidos Australia, AUD $45.9M, August 2025) |
| LOE2 | Hardware procurement — sensors, effectors, C2 |
| **LOE3** | **Standing Offer Panel — site assessment, planning & services** |

**LOE3 is the primary market for this product.** It enables individual Defence bases and whole-of-government sites to be assessed and tendered separately through panel members.

### Key LOE3 Panel Members (Confirmed as of March 2026)

The panel has approximately 29 members across two categories:

- **Category 1 (Services/Assessment):** Nova Systems, and others — scoped for site assessments, threat analysis, solution design, risk/mitigation strategies
- **Category 2 (Solutions Providers):** DroneShield, Dedrone, Anduril, Leidos, L3Harris, RTX, Saab, Cubic Defense, and others — hardware, software, C2, and support under a Capability as a Service (CaaS) model

## 3. Feasibility Assessment

### Overall Verdict: Moderate-to-High Feasibility

The concept is viable with the right sequencing. The key factors:

### Factors Working in Your Favour

- **Market timing is excellent** — LOE3 activity is just beginning to generate site-by-site work; you are not late
- **Genuine product gap** — no independent, vendor-agnostic simulation tool exists in this space in Australia
- **Technical achievability** — assuming technical build is solvable, the MVP is a report-output pipeline, not a full interactive platform
- **Warm access** — employment at a panel member company provides direct market intelligence and a known first customer
- **Sovereign preference** — Australian Government procurement actively prefers Australian SMEs

### Key Challenges

| Challenge | Severity | Mitigation |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| No established accreditation (DISP) | Medium | Achievable; entry-level DISP does not require prior defence track record |
| Long defence sales cycles (12–24 months) | High | Sell to your employer first; target civilian market in parallel |
| Data credibility (simulation accuracy) | High | Document assumptions rigorously; find a technical validator |
| Civilian countermeasure legal ambiguity | Medium | Focus on detection planning for civilian; full system for defence |
| Competition from in-house vendor tools | Medium | Vendor-agnostic positioning is the differentiator |

### DISP Membership — What's Required

The Defence Industry Security Program (DISP) is required for classified defence work. Key requirements:

- Registered Australian business entity (ABN)
- Financially solvent
- A director/senior executive who can obtain an Australian Personnel Security Clearance (to serve as Chief Security Officer)
- A Security Officer with clearance
- Implementation of appropriate physical, cyber, and personnel security measures
- There is **no membership fee**, but implementation costs vary

**Important nuance:** If your simulation tool uses only unclassified data (commercial terrain, publicly available sensor specs, open-source RF propagation modelling), you may be able to operate for civilian customers without DISP. DISP becomes mandatory only when engaging on classified Defence contracts.

**Clearance processing times (current):**
- Baseline: ~20 working days
- NV1: ~70 working days
- NV2: ~100 working days

## 4. Business Model & Pricing

### Structure: Dual Revenue Streams

Your product operates at the intersection of professional services and software licensing. Price both components.

---

#### Component 1 — Per-Engagement Service Fee

Charged for each site simulation engagement as part of a site survey/proposal process.

| Site Complexity | Effort Estimate | Per-Engagement Fee |
|---|---|---|
| Small base / single perimeter | 3–5 days | AUD $15,000–$30,000 |
| Medium base / multiple zones | 5–10 days | AUD $30,000–$60,000 |
| Large / complex installation | 10–20 days | AUD $60,000–$120,000 |

*Travel costs for remote bases should be itemised separately.*

**Pricing rationale:** Senior defence technology specialists bill at AUD $1,800–$2,500/day. As a company delivering a software-enabled specialist service, you bill at the firm rate, not the individual rate. Panel members can absorb $20,000–$80,000 per engagement when the site contract they're bidding on is worth $500K–$5M+.

**Minimum price floor: AUD $15,000.** Below this, you lose credibility and leave money on the table.

---

#### Component 2 — Software Licence Fee

For Panel members who want ongoing or multi-site access without re-engaging you each time:

- **Annual licence per Panel member:** AUD $50,000–$150,000/year (scaled by usage volume and exclusivity terms)
- **Per-site licence (embedded in their proposal):** AUD $5,000–$15,000 per site — Panel member wraps this into their own pricing to Defence

### Key Commercial Terms to Negotiate as a Subcontractor

- **License, don't sell** — retain IP ownership; grant a licence to use
- **Non-exclusive preferred** — preserve your right to sell to other Panel members
- **IP ownership is non-negotiable** — if a Panel member wants to co-develop, get legal advice first
- **Payment terms** — 30 days from the prime's invoice to you, not 30 days after Defence pays the prime
- **Flow-down clauses** — have an Australian defence contracts lawyer review any subcontract before signing

## 5. Build Timeline — Speedrun Strategy

### Assumptions
- Technical background: capable of directing builds, not writing code from scratch
- Build approach: Claude Code as primary development tool
- MVP definition: A professional report output that a Panel member can include in a proposal
- LiDAR site mapping: outsourced to a drone survey company

### Total Timeline to First Revenue: ~22 Weeks (~5–6 Months)

---

### Phase 1 — Data & Knowledge Foundation (Weeks 1–4)

This is the highest-leverage phase. The quality of your threat and sensor data is what makes your output credible.

**Threat data to compile:**
- UAS specifications for NATO Class 1 and 2 targets (sub-25kg commercial and modified commercial drones)
- Sources: NATO STANAG documents, CUAS industry white papers, Jane's summaries, academic RF propagation literature

**Sensor/effector data to compile:**
- Detection ranges, beam widths, frequencies, and blind spots for LOE3 Panel systems
- Acacia Cortex C2, Department 13 sensors, EOS Slinger geometry, Dedrone sensor specs
- Much of this is in public datasheets — you don't need classified specs for a planning tool

**Key decisions to make:**
- **Propagation modelling approach:** True RF propagation (complex, accurate) vs. simplified line-of-sight + range-based model (good enough for site planning, much faster to build). *For MVP: simplified is defensible and significantly faster.*
- **Output format:** Study 2–3 publicly available defence site survey reports (some via AusTender or FOI). Design your output to slot directly into a Panel member's proposal format.

**Deliverables:** Threat/sensor data schema; output template; assumptions document structure

---

### Phase 2 — MVP Build (Weeks 5–14)

Built with Claude Code as primary tool; you as technical director and architect.

**Weeks 5–7: Data pipeline & site ingestion**
- Input handler for LiDAR point cloud data (LAS/LAZ format — agree this with your survey partner before they fly)
- Terrain model generation (Open3D, PDAL, or raster-based approach)
- Site boundary and zone definition interface (web map with polygon drawing)

**Weeks 8–10: Coverage & gap modelling engine**
- Sensor placement simulation: place sensor at point X, calculate coverage arc given terrain occlusion and rated range
- Multi-sensor overlay: coverage zone intersections and gap identification
- Threat corridor modelling: given approach vectors typical for the site, identify exposed zones

> **Shortcut:** Consider wrapping an existing open-source tool (SPLAT!, Radio Mobile, or QGIS terrain analysis plugins) rather than rebuilding from scratch. This saves 3–4 weeks on the coverage engine.

**Weeks 11–13: Configuration recommendation logic**
- Rules-based recommender: given site geometry and threat profile, suggest sensor/effector type and placement
- Gap scoring: quantify coverage percentage, dead zones, response time windows
- "What-if" comparison: Config A vs Config B

**Week 14: Report generation**
- PDF report pipeline: maps, coverage statistics, recommendations, assumptions
- *This is where Claude Code genuinely excels — templated professional report generation is highly achievable*

---

### Phase 3 — Validation & Credibility Package (Weeks 15–18)

This is what turns a prototype into something you can put in front of a Panel member.

- **Sanity-check against known sites:** Run your tool against RAAF Darwin (Anduril deployment is public) as a validation baseline
- **Find a technical validator:** One retired ADF force protection or C-UAS specialist who can review your methodology. Even informal endorsement is significant for credibility.
- **Write your assumptions document:** Explicitly document what your model does and does not account for. Acknowledging limitations builds more trust than hiding them.
- **Produce one full sample report** for a fictional or anonymised site — this becomes your primary sales document

---

### Phase 4 — First Commercial Conversation (Weeks 18–22)

- Approach LOE3 Panel members directly — Nova Systems, DroneShield, Dedrone, Acacia, Department 13
- Pitch: *"When you win a site engagement, we can deliver a simulation-backed configuration report in 5 days. Here's a sample."*
- Target outcome: Letter of Intent or a paid pilot on a real upcoming site survey

---

| Phase | Duration | Key Milestone |
|---|---|---|
| Data & knowledge foundation | 4 weeks | Data schema complete, output format defined |
| MVP build | 10 weeks | Working pipeline producing a report |
| Validation & credibility | 4 weeks | Sample report + assumptions document |

## 6. Navigating Your Current Employment

### The Conflict of Interest Problem

You work for a company that is on the LOE3 panel and pursuing LOE3 site engagements, with connections to Department 13. Building a competing or related product — even on personal time — creates real legal and ethical risk.

### What Your Employment Contract Likely Contains

Most Australian defence industry employment contracts include:

- **IP assignment clauses** — anything you create *related to your employer's business* may legally belong to them, even if built at home and in your own time
- **Conflict of interest restrictions** — competing business activities, even outside work hours
- **Confidentiality obligations** — covering not just documents but knowledge, methodologies, and approaches gained through work
- **Post-employment restraints** — non-competes, non-solicitation of clients

> **The "outside work hours" protection is weaker than people assume.** Australian courts assess *what* you did and whether it conflicts with your employer's business interests — not *when* you did it.

### Department 13 Is a Specific Landmine

Your employer is in active discussions with Department 13. If you are simultaneously building a tool that incorporates Department 13's sensor data to serve the same market your employer is pursuing, that is a serious conflict regardless of your intentions.

### Your Options

| Option | Risk Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Disclose and negotiate** — seek written consent from employer | Low (if approved) | Written consent immunises you; risk is they say no or claim the idea |
| **Wait until you leave** — use current time for research only | Very Low | Slowest path but cleanest |
| **Get legal advice first** | Low | AUD $400–$800 for 1–2 hours with an employment lawyer is the best money you'll spend |
| **Proceed quietly** | Very High | Not recommended — Australian defence industry is small and interconnected |

### What You Can Do Right Now (Legally)

The following activities are generally safe regardless of IP clauses, as they involve public information and general industry knowledge:

- Reading and synthesising publicly available threat data, NATO documents, sensor specs
- Studying LOE3 public announcements, AusTender releases, defence procurement structures
- Designing methodology and architecture (in your head, in personal notes)
- Researching the market, competitors, and pricing

**Do not:** Write code, build tools, or engage with potential customers while this conflict exists without legal clearance.

## 7. Selling to Your Own Company

### Why This Is the Cleanest Path (If Done Right)

Assuming no IP clause covers related-industry work and you build on your own time/systems:

- Your employer is a **known, warm buyer** — no cold sales cycle
- You understand exactly what they need because you work on the bids
- They are already motivated (pursuing LOE3 activities)
- A successful internal sale gives you a **reference customer and case study** for selling to other Panel members

### How to Structure It

**License, don't sell.** Never sell the IP outright — that ends your business before it starts. Push for:

- A **non-exclusive licence** (they can use it; so can others) — strongly preferred
- Or a **time-limited exclusive** (12 months maximum, then non-exclusive)
- **Ongoing licence or per-engagement fee**, not a one-time payment

**Put it in writing** — even with your employer. Especially with your employer. A software licence agreement covering:
- What they can use it for
- What they cannot (no sublicensing, no modifying, no claiming ownership)
- Payment terms
- What happens if you leave the company

**Price it properly.** Don't discount because they're your employer — that sets a bad precedent for every future customer conversation.

### The Conversation to Have

Approach the right decision-maker (whoever controls BD/procurement, not just your line manager):

> *"I'm developing a site-specific cUAS simulation tool in my own time. Given your LOE3 pipeline, I think it would be valuable for your bids. I'd like to discuss a licensing arrangement."*

**Listen carefully for:**
- Do they immediately claim it should belong to the company? *(Red flag — possible IP conflict even without a formal clause)*
- Are they genuinely interested? *(Good — validates market fit)*
- Do they want to buy the IP outright? *(Be ready to decline; explain the licence model)*
- Do they want to co-develop? *(Get legal advice before agreeing — co-development muddies IP ownership significantly)*

### Revised Timeline via This Path

| Phase | Duration | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Legal check + contract review | 1 week | Confirm no IP clause issues |
| Preliminary employer conversation | Week 2 | Gauge interest only; don't commit yet |
| Build MVP | Weeks 3–12 | Building to a known spec — faster |
| Internal demo + licence negotiation | Weeks 13–14 | Real customer in the room |
| First paid engagement | Weeks 15–18 | **Revenue within ~4 months** |

### Critical Longer-Term Point

If your employer wins bids partly because of your tool, you'll eventually face a decision: stay employed while they benefit from your IP at employee-negotiated rates, or leave and build the business properly. The moment you have one successful deployment and a reference case, your negotiating position to raise fees or take the tool to market independently strengthens considerably. Keep that option alive in your licence terms.

## 8. Civilian & Asia-Pacific Market Potential

### Australian Civilian Market

**Verdict: Real opportunity, lower friction than defence, accessible sooner**

The key civilian sectors with immediate cUAS demand in Australia:

| Sector | Opportunity | Access Complexity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Airports | High | Low | 9 major international + ~20 regional; CASA pushing detection capability |
| Mining operations | High | Very Low | Rio Tinto, BHP, Fortescue — industrial espionage, activist drone threat; no DISP required |
| Correctional facilities | Medium-High | Low | Drone contraband incidents rising sharply; state government buyers |
| Critical infrastructure | High | Low-Medium | Power stations, LNG terminals, data centres |
| Events/stadiums | Medium | Low | Temporary deployments; different simulation requirements |

**The regulatory constraint on civilian work:** Australia's countermeasure authority for civilians (jamming, spoofing, hard-kill) remains legally murky outside of law enforcement. Civilian site simulation will therefore focus more on detection coverage than effector placement. This makes civilian engagements slightly smaller in scope but easier to sell and faster to deliver.

4. Expand to Southeast Asia via Australian/US prime contractor relationships

**The trigger for international expansion:** One case study that demonstrates your tool identified a coverage gap that a manual survey missed, or measurably reduced sensor procurement cost on a real site. That story travels internationally on its own.
